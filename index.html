Who's running to face Susan Collins?

Maine Democrats are selecting a new Senate nominee through a first-of-its-kind party convention. Here's who's in the running and where they stand, issue by issue.

This is moving fast. No nominee has been chosen yet — the convention hasn't happened. The candidates below have declared as of July 10, 2026. Expect this field to narrow.

How the replacement gets picked

Maine has never done this before at this scale — here's what's actually been confirmed so far.

A first-of-its-kind nominating convention

The Maine Democratic Party will hold a one-day nominating convention on Saturday, July 25, at the Cross Insurance Center in Bangor, starting at 9 a.m. It's the first time Maine Democrats have used a convention at this scale to replace a statewide nominee, rather than a traditional primary.

500 delegates elected proportionally by county committees
~100 additional votes from the party's state committee
601 total voting delegates at the convention

To get on the ballot at the convention, candidates must file a Declaration of Intent with the party chair by 5 p.m. on July 15 — including a headshot, a 150-word statement of intent, and a 300-word statement on their vision — then submit at least 500 valid signatures from registered Democrats (with a minimum of 50 from each of at least 8 counties) by 11:59 p.m. on July 21. Only Mainers registered as Democrats as of the June 9 primary can serve as delegates.

How the vote works: all certified candidates appear on the first ballot. The top 5 vote-getters advance to a second round; after that, the last-place finisher is eliminated each round until one candidate wins a majority. Delegates arrive unpledged — free to decide after hearing candidates speak at the convention, which will be livestreamed. Maine law requires a replacement nominee to be certified by July 27, 2026.

The candidates

Nine Democrats have launched campaigns as of July 11. The list won't be final until the July 15 filing deadline — some names here may not end up qualifying, and others could still join. Use the picker below to narrow the list, or scroll straight to the issue comparison.

Show:

Shenna Bellows

Maine Secretary of State · former ACLU of Maine director

Bellows previously ran against Collins in 2014 and lost by nearly 37 points — a very different Maine political climate than today. Since then she's spent time in the state Senate and, since 2021, as Maine's top election official, where she drew national attention for ruling Trump ineligible for Maine's 2024 primary ballot (the U.S. Supreme Court later overturned that call). Her positions below come from her recent run for governor, which she just exited, so a Senate-specific platform may still evolve.

Economy
Her "New Deal for Maine" plan: cap utility company rate-of-return at 6%, freeze property taxes for Maine residents, and pay for it by raising taxes on out-of-state second-home owners and adding a 4% millionaire's tax (projected $200M+/year for health care, housing, childcare, and education).
Housing
A "Maine Housing Corps" modeled on the Peace Corps to train construction workers and rehab derelict properties; expanded down-payment support for first-time buyers; eviction-prevention funding for renters.
Healthcare
Backs working toward universal, single-payer healthcare; in the meantime, direct support for hospitals, birthing centers, and community health centers, plus stable funding for school-based health centers and reproductive telehealth.
Immigration
Founding member of the Maine Immigrants' Rights Coalition; as Secretary of State, refused to let ICE use undercover license plates.
Foreign policy
Has not staked out a public position on Israel/Gaza — her campaign launch focused entirely on economic issues. Previously signed a 2023 state proclamation marking the 75th anniversary of Israel's founding while serving in the Secretary of State's office.
Record
Eight years leading Maine's ACLU chapter, including marriage-equality work; more recently a visible check on the Trump administration from the Secretary of State's office.

Troy Jackson

Former Maine Senate President · fifth-generation logger, Allagash

Jackson campaigned for governor alongside Bernie Sanders and built deep labor ties over two decades in Augusta. He's a former Republican who became a Sanders voter in 2016, a union member (Painters/IUPAT and Machinists/IAM), and helped lead a 1998 blockade of the Canadian border protesting the underpricing of Maine loggers' labor.

Labor
As Senate President, fought to protect union jobs, pushed prescription-drug cost relief, and championed a bill giving loggers/wood haulers the right to bargain collectively; endorsed by more than 40 Maine labor unions and the Maine AFL-CIO. He also sponsored the law establishing universal free school meals for Maine K-12 students, backed paid family and medical leave, and championed expanded child care access.
Sexual violence prevention
Lead sponsor of LD1727 (2022), a student- and survivor-written law requiring campuses to provide free counseling/advocacy services, designate a confidential advocate, run biannual campus climate surveys, and guarantee amnesty for students reporting assault — making Maine the 6th state to pass this kind of "Every Voice" legislation.
Healthcare
Supports a public option toward Medicare for All; wants to lower prescription drug prices, protect reproductive care access, and put a moratorium on private equity firms buying up health care practices.
Housing
Accelerate new housing construction, bar private equity from buying up mobile home parks, and create a statewide housing appeals board to cut permitting red tape.
Immigration
Has pledged to pursue legal action against ICE for warrantless entry and appoint a public safety commissioner focused on holding federal immigration agents accountable.
Foreign policy
Says "anybody with eyes and a heart" can see Israel is committing genocide in Gaza and has pledged to "never vote in favor of US taxpayer-funded military aid to Israel." Has drawn backing from Sen. Bernie Sanders' Our Revolution and Rep. Ro Khanna on this basis.
Abortion
As Senate President, championed LD 1619 (2023), which removed Maine's ban on abortion later in pregnancy and made Maine the first state since Dobbs to address that restriction legislatively — one of the more expansive abortion-access laws passed by any state. This marks a shift from votes he took as a state senator representing a more conservative district in the early 2010s, including support for a 24-hour waiting period and an informed-consent requirement; he has said he would not vote to restrict abortion rights in Congress.
LGBTQ+ rights
Was the only Democratic state senator to vote against same-sex marriage in 2009, a vote he has since called "the worst vote I ever took," and said he supported marriage equality when it passed by ballot statewide in 2012. He backed LD 227 (2024), a shield law protecting patients and providers of gender-affirming care from out-of-state legal action.

His record shows real movement over time — from a mixed early-2010s voting record on abortion and same-sex marriage to championing some of the more expansive reproductive-rights and LGBTQ-protection laws Maine has passed. Whether that shift resolves the concerns some in the current coalition have about his earlier record is something voters will weigh for themselves.

Dr. Nirav Shah

Former director, Maine CDC

Shah ran Maine's COVID-19 response and got high marks for it. He won the most first-choice votes in June's gubernatorial primary before losing under ranked-choice voting after other candidates coordinated to consolidate votes against him. He's originally from Wisconsin, which some see as a liability in a state where roots matter.

Healthcare
His Senate site says he's "fighting for Medicare for All"; his four-part governor platform ("fix housing, fund healthcare, feed kids, fuel growth") included capping deductibles/copays/out-of-pocket costs and letting the state negotiate down prescription drug prices.
Housing
Wants urgent increases in housing production, faster permitting, expanded financing, and a first-time-homebuyer program; notably, his platform has not supported requiring landlords to accept Section 8 vouchers or a rental registry, which drew criticism from tenant advocates.
Economy
Says billionaires and corporations should "pay their fair share"; frames himself as a technocratic outsider willing to work with anyone with good ideas, "no matter what party they come from."
Energy
Wants to modernize the grid, expand efficiency programs, and prevent winter price spikes; as governor candidate proposed a public utility scoreboard to track reliability and ratepayer impact.
Foreign policy
Supports a full arms embargo on Israel, calls its conduct in Gaza genocide, and says he won't accept AIPAC money or endorsement — a notably more critical stance than he took as a gubernatorial candidate, when he didn't address the issue and drew AIPAC-linked outside spending.
Record
No prior elected office — his profile is technocratic/public-health rather than legislative. Critics have also pointed to his handling of a 2015 Legionnaires' outbreak while leading Illinois public health under Gov. Bruce Rauner, when some Democratic senators called for his resignation.

Dan Kleban

Co-founder, Maine Beer Company · attorney by training

The only non-politician with real traction. Kleban co-founded Maine Beer Company in Freeport in 2009 after being laid off during the Great Recession, building it into one of the state's largest breweries. He briefly ran for this Senate seat in 2025 before dropping out to back Mills, and re-entered the race in July.

Framing
"Sick and tired of a system that's been rigged by corporate interests" — leaning into anti-establishment, anti-Washington-insider, anti-"New York City consultant" messaging. Says his focus is "practical policies, not just outrage."
Economy / affordability
Campaign centers on cost-of-living relief — housing, health care, groceries, electric bills — which he argues Collins has stopped prioritizing after nearly 30 years in office.
Labor practices (business record)
Built Maine Beer Company around paying living wages, covering full employee health care, offering retirement benefits, and donating 1% of gross sales to climate/conservation nonprofits — a record he points to as a preview of his priorities.
Record
No voting record to scrutinize, for better or worse; his only prior campaign was the few weeks he spent running for this same seat in 2025.

Valli Geiger

Maine State Representative, Rockland · nurse, former mayor

Geiger is serving her third term in the Maine House (Labor Committee; Energy, Utilities and Technology Committee), was Rockland's mayor for a year, and worked as a nurse and later a health-policy administrator before running for office. Her candidacy status is less settled than others in this field — as of mid-July she'd expressed interest and been discussed as a contender but had not filed formal declaration paperwork.

Healthcare
Nursing and hospice/health-administration background gives her a direct professional stake in the issue.
Energy/climate
Built her own passive-solar, net-zero home; holds a master's in sustainable design; sits on the legislature's energy committee.
Labor
Serves on the Maine House Labor Committee, giving her a working knowledge of state-level labor policy.
Record
Six years on the Rockland City Council including a year as mayor, plus three terms in the Maine House — the most local-government experience in the field.

Jordan Wood

Former congressional chief of staff · Lewiston native

Wood, 36, was Chief of Staff to Rep. Katie Porter and says he was barricaded in the Capitol on January 6. He originally ran for this Senate seat before switching to the ME-02 House primary after Rep. Jared Golden announced he wouldn't seek reelection, finishing third; he's now back in the Senate race. He'd be the first openly gay senator from Maine and has published one of the most detailed platforms in the field.

Democracy reform
Overturn Citizens United, ban congressional stock trading, end partisan gerrymandering, add Supreme Court term limits, and bar members of Congress from taking corporate PAC or lobbyist money.
Healthcare
Supports Medicare for All as a single-payer system while preserving private insurance for those who want it; in the meantime, a public option, expanded ACA subsidies, Medicare dental/vision/hearing, and Big Pharma price negotiation.
Housing
Stop hedge funds from buying single-family homes, ban algorithmic rent-fixing, anti-rent-gouging laws, accelerate new construction, defer property taxes up to 5 years for first-time buyers.
Childcare
Cap out-of-pocket childcare at $10/day per child for families earning under $250,000, funded through federal grants to providers.
Taxes
Cut the federal rate to 1% on a household's first $50,000 earned, paid for via an "Ultra-Millionaire Tax" and closing corporate offshoring loopholes.
Foreign policy
Says Israel has committed war crimes in Gaza but stops short of "genocide," citing concern about the term's link to rising antisemitism; supports restricting offensive weapons sales with conditions and won't take AIPAC money.
Ethics pledge
No corporate PAC, AIPAC, or lobbyist money; won't trade stocks in office; won't become a lobbyist after; publishes his meeting calendar; holds a town hall in every county annually.

David Costello

2024 Democratic Senate nominee (lost to Angus King) · Old Town

Costello already ran statewide for Senate in 2024, losing to independent incumbent Angus King. Grandson of shoe-mill and railroad workers, he spent 30+ years in government — deputy/acting secretary of Maryland's Department of the Environment, USAID crisis work in Cambodia, Haiti, Bosnia, and Kosovo, and a top aide to Secretary of State Bill Diamond in Maine in the 1990s.

Democracy reform
His signature issue: campaign finance reform, ending the Senate filibuster and gerrymandering, term limits for the House and Senate (24 years) and for federal judges including the Supreme Court, and abolishing the Electoral College.
Climate
Directed climate-focused foundation projects and served as interim climate/clean-energy director at the Natural Resources Council of Maine; wants an aggressive, science-based national climate plan with a panel to track progress.
Civil rights
Campaign platform includes a dedicated plank on "civil rights, including women's reproductive rights, and public safety."
Guns
Describes himself as a lifelong hunter and gun owner who takes gun safety seriously, and favors an "all of the above" approach to reducing gun violence.
Foreign policy
Says Israel had "a right and an obligation" to respond to the October 7 attacks but calls the scale of its military response "indefensible"; would have opposed offensive weapons sales, supports a two-state solution, and says the U.S. should support Israel's political opposition to Netanyahu.
Record
The most institutionally experienced candidate in the field by far, though also a two-time statewide candidate (this Senate seat in 2024 and now again).

Paige Loud

Social worker, 29 · Old Town

Loud ran in the Democratic primary for Maine's 2nd Congressional District this year and came in fourth with about 10% of the vote. She's the youngest declared candidate, self-describes as "unabashedly progressive," and is running an explicitly small-dollar, no-corporate-PAC campaign — she says her donations fund things like her staff's pay and gas for her own car rather than outside consultants.

Healthcare
Backs universal healthcare as a human right — comprehensive coverage (medical, dental, vision, mental health, long-term care) based on need, plus federal investment in rural/urban public hospitals and telehealth access.
Economy
Raise and inflation-index the federal minimum wage, close tax loopholes for the wealthy and large corporations, strengthen union rights, and value caregiving work with fair pay.
Housing
"Housing First" approach treating homelessness as a public health issue; a federal tax on corporate/private-equity ownership of single-family homes to curb speculative buying.
Food security
Expand SNAP, fund universal school meals, and push antitrust enforcement against grocery-chain consolidation.
Foreign policy
Frames it around human rights and self-determination — names Palestine, Iran, Ukraine, Venezuela, and Greenland specifically — and opposes what she calls militarism over diplomacy.

Of this whole field, Loud has the most fully public, written-out issue platform — most others are still working from campaign-trail statements rather than a published set of positions.

Andrea LaFlamme

Public health professor · union chapter president

LaFlamme was already running a write-in campaign for this seat before Platner's exit — she wasn't on the June primary ballot but campaigned anyway, including chalking a message about abortion rights outside Susan Collins' house in 2022. She teaches nutrition, health economics, and women's health at Maine community colleges and is the only candidate in the field who's an elected union leader — chapter president of MSEA-SEIU Local 1989, representing adjunct professors statewide. She was also the youngest-ever board president of the Mabel Wadsworth Center, a Bangor reproductive health clinic.

Reproductive rights
Wants to restore Title X family-planning funding (unchanged since 2015), oppose reinstating the "domestic gag rule," and pass federal right-to-contraception legislation.
Labor
As union chapter president, recently secured 4% annual raises for adjunct professors; backs the Paycheck Fairness Act and a federal Raise the Wage Act.
Economy
Wants to close the gender pay gap through expanded caregiving tax credits and more progressive tax rates.
Record
No prior elected office outside her union role; frames her lack of a political résumé as a feature, arguing Congress needs more teachers and health professionals rather than lawyers.

More names could still surface before the July 15 filing deadline.
